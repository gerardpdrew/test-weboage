The Grand Canyon

Carved by the Colorado River over millions of years, the Grand Canyon stretches 277 miles (446 km) through northwestern Arizona. It reaches depths of over a mile (1,600 m) and widths of up to 18 miles (29 km), exposing one of the most complete geological records on Earth.

Geology

The canyon's walls reveal nearly two billion years of Earth's history, laid bare by the relentless erosion of the Colorado River beginning around 5–6 million years ago. The rock layers — called strata — are read from top to bottom like pages of a book, with the youngest rocks at the rim and the oldest deep in the inner gorge.

The forces that shaped the canyon include river erosion, freeze-thaw weathering, wind, and the gradual uplift of the Colorado Plateau, which raised the land and steepened the river's gradient, accelerating the cutting process.

Major Rock Layers

Kaibab Limestone ~270 million years — marine fossils, forms the rim
Coconino Sandstone ~275 million years — ancient desert dunes
Hermit Formation ~280 million years — red shale, fern fossils
Supai Group ~285–315 million years — red sandstone & shale
Redwall Limestone ~340 million years — cliffs stained red by iron
Muav Limestone ~505 million years — early Cambrian seas
Bright Angel Shale ~515 million years — trilobite fossils
Vishnu Basement Rocks ~1.7–1.8 billion years — metamorphic core
